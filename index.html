Leaving the Poison Orchard: Navigating the Right to Privacy in the US

By: Saraphina Forman. CS 1805. Timothy Edgar. March 13, 2026. See works cited.




Intro. We are surrounded by discourse about balancing the right to privacy with the "greater good”—the country might need to overstep to protect you, or perhaps protect others from you. Studying the history of wiretapping and bugging provides context for how public opinion on surveillance technology has shifted. We frame this discussion about when surveillance should be legal. However, this can lead us down can lead us down hypothetical riddles that are difficult to emerge from with clarity. Here, I consider stepping outside the legal framework to exit the paradox, thinking about privacy not in terms of US law but in terms of what law forecloses. Cold War surveillance shows how negotiating legality inherently protects imperial interests and forecloses the possibility of true liberation. Why exactly is the legal framework an issue?
Bad news , good news.
First, consider the “fruit of the poisonous tree” doctrine, which is a metaphor in law conveying the idea that, if evidence is tainted, results obtained using that evidence will also be tainted. Effectively, this idea "extends the exclusionary rule to make evidence inadmissible in court if it was derived from evidence that was illegally obtained” (Cornell Law School). However, if we truly embrace this sort of framework, doesn’t that undermine US law itself, considering the fact that the US is at heart a poison fruit as it is built upon the labor of enslaved people and Indigenous genocide? Of course, we can’t extend the metaphor to the point of discarding all of human history due to all its little wrongdoings long ago, nor is this to make a surface-level discrediting of the character we seek to critique. But in this case, the recentness of the situation at hand and the direct ties to its legal systems created intimately from this poison wood are quite tangible; the effects of the poison are not metaphorical in the US. Given that, it’s worth taking a look at what happens after you discard the fruit of the poisonous tree, after you get up from under it? Not just the evidence, but the laws that branched off from slavery laws. What do you eat?
The poison tree pt. 2. Black anarchist William C. Anderson summarizes the issues with trying to have a conversation about systemic justice within a reformist framework: "When I speak of abolition..I am not focused on one aspect of a despicable situation as much as I am questioning the situation as a whole. The United States does not have problems, the United States is the problem.” We may toss out this apple or that one, take the ones that are slightly less poisonous, but at the end of the day, compromise just remixes the enduring death wish. Furthermore, gains are passing. We have to go through the state’s channels and speak in its language for every reformist win, and that undercuts the thrust of the thing. Legal reform on surveillance is telling the state that it can decide what it’s allowed to do. It's a tautology. It’s letting the state decide what kind of injustices we deserve. Essentially, the state is deciding how much power it will take over us.
The poison tree pt. 3.This is not the cynical argument of "the problem is giving the state power to decide its reach, because if power is available, an entity will always take more." It's more abstract—the state is the embodiment of power, so even laws limiting its power are an expression of its power; moreover, the state will only "follow” its laws when it wants to anyway.
The poison tree pt 4.The idea of crime is very much tied up with blackness in the carceral state that is the US. For example, in the 1857 Dred Scott Supreme Court decision, the rationale said "The words ‘people of the United States’ and ‘citizens’ are synonymous terms,” and it went on to say, when it came to Black people, "We think they are not, and that they are not included, and were not intended to be included, under the word ‘citizens’ in the Constitution, and can therefore claim none of the rights and privileges which that instrument provides for and secures to citizens of the United States” (Taney, cited by Anderson). These effects carry over to today and the carceral state that permeates the "real” state in institutionalized racism. Indeed, Anderson reminds us that no accumulation of gains exempts Black people from the violence of the carceral state. Given this racialization of innocence, we must be particularly watchful of a reformist tilt to our conversations about the civil rights movement. Anderson states that "The civil rights movement is selectively remembered in popular and state-sponsored retellings of history, reducing a complex phenomenon to favored leaders, organizations, and actions. The distorted focus on reformist efforts creates a battle within history itself.” History around surveillance, specifically within the civil rights movement and communism, then, can thus prove fertile ground for thinking about legality and offer a fresh perspective on surveillance, which is sometimes analyzed too narrowly.

This project is not meant to avoid taking a crack at the "when should surveillance be illegal?" paradox, but rather it claims a genuine deficiency of turning it into a riddle or intellectual thought experiment. It is worth addressing this recurrent rhetorical device head-on: "yeah wiretapping’s bad, but what if there’s a terrorist who's going to kill 50 people, isn't it worth it to wiretap them then?” This line of thinking is reminiscent of other thought lines of arguments for things like torture, because it is trying to leverage economic cost-benefit analysis to facilitate compromising on civil liberties. (It’s interesting that surveillance may seem much less intense than something like torture, but often provokes a similar sort of disgust.) For example, the government’s inaction in stopping King’s outlandish surveillance " appears to have stemmed from a belief that it was safer to permit the FBI to conduct the investigation than to stop the Bureau and run the risk of charges that the FBI was being muzzled for political reasons” (Church Committee 92). With the muzzle on, who is the FBI missing out on biting? The common "national security threat” exception to surveillance bills is another invocation of this hypothetical Bad Guy, this figure that’s so dangerous even in his spectral, non-existent form that he is worth the harm of surveilling citizens if you can find him. From the very fact that the government's assessment of who constitutes a national security threat has been off base in the most conservative judgment, the policy is not working. Beyond that, the hypothetical figure is a fear tactic, serving to justify violence. This figure plays the role of the class "Other” that helps shepherd everyone else into line against them.
The Perfect Victim. Cold war surveillance, and even more so, our retelling of its rationales and re-evaluation of its legitimacy, is laden with "perfect victim” mythology–the idea that if you are not pure, "sympathetic,” relatable, and palatable to conventional tastes, then the crime done unto you is your fault. This creates a black and white scenario where only a completely innocent victim is worth protecting, while a victim who fought back or had any prior involvement is disqualified from justice. Anything less than perfect passivity voids the claim. The invocation of the "good victim,” who in these surveillance cases, actually takes the form of the "respectable criminal” who didn’t deserve the clumsy attacks of the state, implicitly preserves the "bad victim” who isn’t worth protecting or "legitimate criminal” who forgoes their rights. And of course, if there is only justice guaranteed for a subject that fits a specific ideal, there is no justice. The state and its media can always deem "non-citizen” subjects and dissidents as the criminal Other — that's what it was built to do. Even an absolute wiretapping ban, which is unlikely, illustrates this: it would still only protect those the state already recognizes as rights-bearing subjects. Everyone else–and the definition of who everyone else is–remains at the whim of the state; the government, as history shows, is not bound by its own laws anyway. This perfect victim narrative is especially noticeable in the Cold War surveillance FBI cases of both Judith Coplon and Martin Luther King, Jr. Loch Johnson describes the government project COINTELPRO: "The Bureau operations involved not only spying against Americans but also secret efforts to destroy the jobs, marriages, and reputations of individuals whose only sin had been to engage in activities protected by the First Amendment, such as protesting against the war in Vietnam or joining the civil rights movement” (Loch Johnson). Religious word choices like "sin” and key ideas to the middle class white nuclear family– "jobs” "marriage” and "reputation” are invoked for presumed immediate innocence and sympathy; these are "civilians” who do not deserve surveillance, as opposed to real criminals. The "protests” cited as examples are convenient ones that have been deeply sanitized in their narratives and in pop culture almost unanimously on the "good side.” There is no mention of the less sanitized concurrent movements, such as the Black Panther Party or the Weather Underground (although these have surely received their fair share of aestheticization in certain leftist media spaces, especially in recent times).
Judith Coplon worked briefly for the US and was prosecuted for being a soviet spy, but ultimately got off because the evidence against her was acquired through illegal wiretapping. The official 1950 United States v. Coplon decision uses words common to the religious context, e.g., "consummation” in its report; it echoes the moralism of the early days of US privacy laws, where Benjamin Franklin leveraged enlightenment and Protestant values going back to the foundations of the US. Coplon’s daughter continued to emphasize her mother’s innocence to this day, rationalizing the ample evidence that Coplon was a spy by making it seem palatable to liberals, almost saccharine in its evasiveness. The New York Times quotes her below: “It’s very hair-raising to read about your mother being given a code name and moved around like a chess piece,” the daughter added. "Was she a spy? I think it’s another question that I ask: Was she part of a community that felt that they were going to bring, by their actions, an age of peace and justice and an equal share for all and the abolishing of color lines and class lines?” “If these were things that she actually did, she was not defining them as espionage,” Ms. Socolov continued. "If you feel that what you’re doing answers to a higher ideal, it’s not treason.” To be sure, an extremely clean, pure self-image makes sense as a pragmatic performance of denying guilt, but it hampers the construction of a country that isn't so selective in its justice.
A similar whitewashed narrative surrounds Martin Luther King, Jr., this time casting him as a generic sort of martyr. A Church Committee hearing transcript reads: Mr. ADAMS. There were approximately twenty-five incidents of actions taken [to discredit Dr. King] . . . I see no statutory basis or no basis of justification for the activity. The CHAIRMAN. Was Dr. King, in his advocacy of equal rights for black citizens, advocating a course of action that in the opinion of the FBI constituted a crime? Mr. ADAMS. No, sir. The CHAPMAN. He was preaching non-violence was he not, as a method of achieving equal rights for black citizens? Mr. ADAMS. That’s right . . . Now as far as the activities which you are asking about, the discrediting, I know of no basis for that and I will not attempt to justify it. Why is the concept of "non-violence” so fundamental to sympathy towards King? It is the liberation of Black people that is the true threat to the state whose interests the FBI defends; "violence,” like "crime,” has long been defined in connection with blackness in the first place so as to rationalize the abuse of King regardless. It is the retelling of this narrative where the surveillance was a "mistake,” a wrongdoing on this innocent man who just had a dream, that tries to obscure the true role of the state, which was functioning as intended. Therefore, legal cases like King’s surveillance (Liberal narrative: he was a nonviolent man, how dare they surveil him!) and Coplon (Her family: if she was doing communism, she was doing it with morally good intentions; therefore she wasn't guilty) are counterproductive. Imperial concessions are not worth celebrating nor even accepting. As poet and theorist Fred Moten says, "To refuse the poisonous gift of an autonomy or a citizenship or a right that is always withheld is also to refuse the tortured logic that apprehends racialisation… as an enduring social death.” So...

They never generated legal meaning anyway.
Jurisgenesis. Robert Cover’s term jurisgenesis, taken up by Moten, is useful—this is the "capacity to create legal meaning” which "requires no state,” from which the courts select and suppress. Jurisgenesis transcends the law "as written and determined by any given legal system” (David Lloyd, citing Moten and Cover). Thus, the reason for courts is not the need for law but "the need to suppress the law, to choose between two or more laws, to impose upon laws a hierarchy. It is the multiplicity of laws, the fecundity of the principle of jurisgenesis, that creates the problem for which the court and the state are the solution” (Moten). Here, we see that meaning proliferates outside the courtroom, in various creative, collective narratives about what privacy is and what kind of culture we maintain for ourselves. The courts just stamp it "official.” For example...
Jurisgenesis pt. 2 Justice Black’s dissenting opinion on the 1967 Katz v. United States case reads "The Court talks about a constitutional ‘right of privacy’ as though there is some constitutional provision or provisions forbidding any law ever to be passed which might abridge the ‘privacy’ of individuals. But there is not.” Justice Black takes a "facts and logic” approach to try to take the wind out of instincts towards justice and discredit legal meanings that aren’t literally enshrined in the courts, but in the process, he admits that a powerful public understanding does not match what’s on file. Note that jurisgenesis does not imply an essential, independent "natural law” latent in the people. The beauty of jurisgenesis is that layers are continually appended. Our ability to have "extra legal meaning” much like extra grammatical language predates an unending oscillation between systemic and anti-systemic by being a parallel, fertile, "endless mutation and differentiation” (Lloyd). Figures such as Lyndon B. Johnson are similarly tasked with upholding a single legal meaning, supposedly coming to ease the confusion of these paradoxical legal contexts with standardization and a dash of social control. Of course, LBJ, described as "nothing if not contradictory,” was himself immersed in multiple legal meanings, to which he expressed split sympathies. Some historians offer a redemptive reading of the stark hypocrisy between LBJ’s colorful statements against wiretapping–“I’m a red-hot one-million-two percent civil liberties man, and I'm just against [wiretappers]”—and his continued allowance of the practice; Walker argues LBJ’s denunciation was never "a promise not to enforce a law” but rather a "legitimate expression of his views and a call for congress to reexamine” the newly-passed 1968 "first federal law authorizing wiretapping” (Walker). Perhaps LBJ's waffling came from an intuition that wiretapping violated civil liberties, but a difficulty following that logic to its conclusion, which would require the recognition of the fugitive. What turmoil! Whatever the case, LBJ ultimately was on the side of the empire, because the threat of the "many worlds” created by jurisgenesis spawns "imperial virtues and the imperial mode of world maintenance.” This maintenance, which is just as energy-intensive as creating worlds, involves force and violence, even if it's often masked through a lower-profile "watching,” as we saw through the FBI’s struggle to surveil King. J. Edgar Hoover, too, was on the jurisgenesis patrol; he "understood better than many others in Washington that public attitudes about privacy and wiretapping were changing rapidly,” and after Senator Long’s congressional hearings on snooping, he described an "undeclared war on privacy.” So, what exactly was happening in the minds of people outside the courtroom?
Jurisgenesis pt 3. In general, meaning-generation was rich, contradictory, and confused. From one perspective, there was real fear about the Soviet Union, especially encouraged by government propaganda. At the same time, throughout US history, people have often had some instinct that their privacy should be respected and surveillance is wrong, despite sometimes the law coming down against it (e.g. the "paroxysm of national anger” at the 1844 postal espionage case, the rallying around Olmstead during prohibition in 1928, the satirical "Some Fun with the FBI” New Yorker article from 1944), the "allegations of domestic spying” that "sent a shock wave…across the nation, and countless political cartoons —even if people they don’t take action corresponding to their outrage (Vincent, McKelway, Abbott, Johnson, Hulme, Block). There's also a media enthrallment with government infringements of privacy that in some ways transcends the power of normative judgements one way or another. Maybe it's because there's this kind of strange voyeurism implicated in an external body looking through people’s private lives. In addition, impedance on privacy attracts attention because it inflames American individualism. This means that part of the overwhelming popularity of pro-privacy arguments may come from a neoliberal, ego-centric position that A) only holds when the citizen sees the surveilled as relatable enough to project onto (read: citizen, human, white) and B) triggers individualist paranoia, e.g. the white anarchist stereotype who goes down countless VPN rabbit holes while isolating themselves from broader shared struggles. However, even this individualist impulse can be harnessed for collective paranoia–used to get people to act more securely in their own communication, to put in the time to understand intentionally opaque technology and learn DIY techniques, gesturing towards more prepared, capable communities. Here, the atomized extremists of the political compass may come together against the centrists who fall further behind in a battle set in a status quo that gets harder to defend with each passing day.
Scandal. The media’s fascination with privacy invasion finds its apotheosis in the modern scandal. Consider the 1972 Watergate scandal, where Richard Nixon "lied to the American people in an attempt to cover up a late-night GOP break-in to the Democratic presidential campaign headquarters” (loch t johnson xiii). As Baudrillard puts it, "the simulacrum is never what hides the truth—it is truth that hides the fact that there is none.” By being a spectacular malfunction of the law, scandal gestures towards a world in which the laws are functioning normally, correctly. Thus, "the denunciation of scandal is always an homage to the law.” In this way, despite the exposure of the scandal and Nixon’s resignation, perhaps the operation may have functioned as a victory for the government in the psychological sphere. Furthermore, the embarrassing exposure of the extent of government surveillance functions to intimidate people. Momentum generated by the outrage at Watergate’s privacy violations died out over the course of the follow-up Church Committee hearings, as TV coverage waned and public interest turned to other things. The scandal and the subsequent punishment brought people through emotional catharsis. Spectacles such as this are performances that simulate conflict and resolution, whether or not there is corresponding political action. While the effects of the carceral state are deeply tangible and violent, in many ways, the court is a performance in service of itself, reproducing its own symbol.
Feeding ourselves. Since legal meaning comes from outside the law anyway, the path forward is not reform but redirection, harnessing extralegal instincts toward collective counter-action and building outside the state. Surveillance is the perfect synecdoche for this: it distills the cost-benefit logic of governance down to how much violation we are willing to absorb in exchange for the promise of security. When Ford expressed fatigue about constantly having to hear America being called the "bad guy” during the Church Committee hearings, he revealed exactly whose comfort the system is built to protect. The scope of this project does not allow for delving deeply into how to fix the situation, so it is ultimately not a prescriptive argument. But in order to avoid being a purely critical essay, I list some principles that may act as salve while we are in a country whose conventional channels for change will not bring the justice we want. This is to be an alternative to legislation.
Opacity. We can utilize our own tools to withhold information. This can involve leaving phones at home, not participating in the like-based economy, and not engaging. This brings to mind the famous quote from writer and theorist Édouard Glissant, who says in Caribbean Discourse, "We demand the right to opacity.” Note that the quote isn't that "I" deserve the right, it's "we," and it refers not only to individuals but to cultures, things, art, people, anything. The collective opacity goes well with collective privacy.
Counter-surveillance. Rather than waiting for our nation’s leadership to tell us what level of privacy violation we should tolerate, where their view of the greater good is not resonant, we can act as non-state community entities within the state that define for ourselves what we see as legal meaning and seek to support it in our own ways. We follow our own jurisgenesis against the suppressive interpretation of the courts. Models for this subversion are groups like the Black Panther Party, which enacted counter-intelligence.
Performance and "trickster” ethos. This leans into the inherently spectacular nature of the court process. Rather than pretending it will have lasting effects in the results it thinks it's enacting as justice, we can take advantage of the public media presence and utilize the platform and attention to communicate our own narratives of the system. This squares with Baudrillard’s idea that people's perceptions become more important than the actual events. Rather than driving ourselves crazy with the fact that we can't escape the cycles of even our own protest of the spectacle becoming a spectacle, we can playfully lean into it. For example, another reading of the Coplon case would be to see her performance of innocence as a parody of the system. Furthermore, she refuses to engage with liberal centrist media, letting the absurdity speak for itself rather than legitimizing the proceedings by explaining herself. "We’ve had all kinds of requests for interviews, for books, but it has been our steady policy to refuse,” her husband said. "Other people are interested in posterity. We’re not” (Roberts).
Community caretaking. This is the classic anarchist idea of protecting ourselves, taking care of ourselves, rather than relying on outside forces such as police or the state more generally. Examples of this are the free breakfast program started by the Black Panther Party, mutual aid, and community libraries. This doesn't necessarily speak to the surveillance-specific issue, but working towards liberation from the state is an important part of preparing oneself for resistance. It’s interesting to consider the idea that we don’t "live” in "capitalism” (or even technofeudalism or what have you) as a physical fact. Power, such as a government being able to decapitate you, is real, but we can also acknowledge, as Étienne de La Boétie argues, that servitude is to some degree voluntary–it is a choice to submit oneself to authority; if not, we may engage in civil disobedience. As they say, the best way to bring about the end of capitalism is to pretend that it’s already here, and see the multiplicity of societies that are already functioning, rather than the capitalist-realist "no alternative” type of mindset.
Alternative infrastructure and means of production. Rather than skirting the technology issue, which is often the default for leftism, we can embrace technology, but instead of relying on mass companies such as Meta, we can work on building community-based networks. An example of this is Peer-to-Peer networks, open-source code, DIY electronics communities, and internet forums, or historical ones such as Bolivian Miners’ Radio or Zapatista early internet use. There are still inherent problems–it’s not like we can pop up with a "leftist” data center complex competing with the "evil ChatGPT” version a few miles over, but there is definitely a long way to go before that point. This embrace of technology, combined with a reduction in consumerism, can be powerful. I hope to explore resistance from a technological standpoint more deeply in future projects.
Harm reduction. This is a pragmatic way of looking at legal systems in the short term. Inconvenient as it may be, dismissing government altogether because one can make an intellectually watertight existential argument against it can be an overly black-and-white view of the situation. This is not reform; we must not give an inch in terms of the radicality of the agenda. However, the idea of harm reduction can be helpful, metaphorically drawing from the concept’s use in substance use contexts, e.g., opiate addiction is a larger struggle, but providing access to clean needles has real effects that address the immediate violence people are facing. When some laws would obviously make a difference in government, like limiting big tech companies from mining us for data or using certain addictive algorithms, we shouldn’t necessarily oppose them. Unless one supports a full accelerationist mindset, but that’s a whole other thing.
