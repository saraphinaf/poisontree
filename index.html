Leaving the Poison Orchard: Navigating the Right to Privacy in the US

We often are surrounded by a seemingly-pressing discourse about how to balance the right to one’s own privacy with the “greater good”--a country might need to overstep in order to protect you, or, perhaps, protect others from you. Studying the history of wiretapping and other political utilizations of technological developments throughout time provides context for the ways our public opinion has changed. We frame this discussion about when surveillance should be legal. However, this can lead us down interpretive hypotheticalriddles that seem difficult to come out of with clarity. I propose that we need to step outside this legal framework to exit the paradox provided by the legal complex we live in, thinking about rights to privacy in terms of natural law rather than US law. We can examine cold war surveillance to look at how the idea of negotiating legality inherently protects imperial interests and forecloses the possibility for true liberation. Why exactly is the legal framework an issue?

First, consider the “fruit of the poisonous tree” doctrine, which is a metaphor in law conveying the idea that, if evidence is tainted, results obtained using that evidence will also be tainted. Effectively, this idea “extends the exclusionary rule to make evidence inadmissible in court if it was derived from evidence that was illegally obtained” (Cornell Law School). However, if we truly embrace this sort of framework, doesn’t that undermine US law itself, considering the fact that the US is at heart a poison fruit as it is built upon the labor of enslaved people and Indigneous genocide? Of course, we can’t extend the metaphor to the point of discarding all of human history due to all its little wrongdoings long ago, nor is this to make a surface-level discrediting of the character we seek to critique. But in this case, the recentness of the situation at hands and the direct ties to its legal systems created intimately from this poison wood is quite tangible, the effects of the poison are not metaphorical in the US. Given that, it’s worth taking a look at what happens after you discard the fruit of the poisonous tree, after you get up from under it? Not just the evidence, but the laws that branched off of slave laws. What do you eat? How do you conceptualize what’s just? Black anarchist William C. Anderson explains the issues with trying to have a conversation about systemic justice with a reformist framework: “When I speak of abolition—that is, the dismantling rather than mere reforming of the institutions that maintain capitalism, white supremacy, and other oppressions—I am not focused on one aspect of a despicable situation as much as I am questioning the situation as a whole. The United States does not have problems, the United States is the problem.” We may toss out this apple or that one, take the ones that are slightly less poisonous, but at the end of the day, “People who insist on compromising with the forces that are determined to exterminate us are only attempting to rearrange the terms of a perpetual crisis.” Furthermore, whatever gains are made can easily be undone. “Everything that we claim as an accomplishment through machinery that our oppressors control can ultimately be undone.” The idea of crime is very much tied up with blackness in the carceral state that is the US. For example, in the 1857 Dred Scott Supreme Court decision, the rationale said “The words ‘people of the United States’ and ‘citizens’ are synonymous terms,” and it went on to say, when it came to Black people, “We think they are not, and that they are not included, and were not intended to be included, under the word ‘citizens’ in the Constitution, and can therefore claim none of the rights and privileges which that instrument provides for and secures to citizens of the United States.” These effects carry over to today and the carceral state that permeates the “real” state in institutionalized racism. Indeed, “No matter how much we achieve, accumulate, or excel at playing the game, we’re always capable of being caged, repressed, brutalized, or killed because we’re Black.” Given this racialization of innocence, we must be particularly watchful of a reformist tilt to our conversations of the civil rights movement. Anderson states that “The civil rights movement is selectively remembered in popular and state-sponsored retellings of history, reducing a complex phenomenon to favored leaders, organizations, and actions. The distorted focus on reformist efforts creates a battle within history itself.” History around surveillance specifically within the civil rights movement and communism(?), then, can thus prove fertile ground for thinking about legality and offer a fresh perspective on surveillance which can often be analyzed too narrowly.

Cold war surveillance, and even more so, our retelling of its rationales and assessment of its legitimacy, is laden with “perfect victim” mythology–the idea that if you are not pure (read: white), “sympathetic” or “relatable,” palatable to conventional tastes, then the crime done unto you is your fault.There is a black and white situation where a completely innocent victim is worth protecting ,whereas a victim with any drop of fighting back or doubt of participation is disqualified from justice. And of course, if there is only justice guaranteed for a specific ideal of subject, there is no justice. This perfect victim narrative is especially noticeable in the cold war surveillance FBI cases of both Judith Coplon and Martin Luther King, Jr. Loch Johnson describes the government project COINTELPRO: “The Bureau operations involved not only spying against Americans but also secret efforts to destroy the jobs, marriages, and reputations of individuals whose only sin had been to engage in activities protected by the First Amendment, such as protesting against the war in Vietnam or joining the civil rights movement” (Loch Johnson). Religious word choices like “sin” and key ideas to the middle class white nuclear family– “jobs” “marriage” and “reputation” are invoked for presumed immediate innocence and sympathy; these are “civilian” citizens who do not deserve surveillance, as opposed to real criminals. The “protests” cited as examples are convenient ones that have been deeply sanitized in their narratives and in pop culture almost unanimously on the “good side.” There is no mention of the less sanitized contemporary movements such as the Black Panther Party or __.

Judith Coplon worked briefly for the US and was prosecuted for being a soviet spy but ultimately got off because the evidence against her was acquired through illegal wiretapping. The official 1950 United States vs Coplon decision uses words common to the religious context e.g. “consummation” in its report; echoes the moralism of the early days of US privacy laws where Benjamin Franklin leveraged enlightenment and protestant values tying back to the Protestantism that underlies the US individual society. Coplon’s daughter emphasizes Coplon’s innocence to this day, rationalizing the ample evidence that Coplon was a spy by making it seem palatable to liberals, almost saccharine in its evasiveness: “It’s very hair-raising to read about your mother being given a code name and moved around like a chess piece,” the daughter added. “Was she a spy? I think it’s another question that I ask: Was she part of a community that felt that they were going to bring, by their actions, an age of peace and justice and an equal share for all and the abolishing of color lines and class lines?” “If these were things that she actually did, she was not defining them as espionage,” Ms. Socolov continued. “If you feel that what you’re doing answers to a higher ideal, it’s not treason.” Just say she was a spy, girl! An extremely clean, pure self image works as a pragmatic performance of denying guilt but it is counterproductive to the effort to build a world that isn't so selective in its justice.
